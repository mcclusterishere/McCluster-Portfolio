What the current site costs the mission

The first line the world reads is misspelled.

The homepage title in Google says “Ecomonic Development.” For a firm whose product is credibility, that one word does real damage every single day.

The fix: it is gone on day one of the rebuild.
The site turns visitors away.

Security settings answer many legitimate visits with “Forbidden.” Shared links show up blank. A door that says go away cannot bring partners in.

The fix: modern hosting with nothing to block and nothing to hack.
Thirteen pages named in code.

NFLC. CFLC. FCDC. Pages named for insiders. A program officer in Washington or a pastor in Houston cannot find the door that is meant for them.

The fix: six clear pages, every program in plain English under one roof.
No way to say yes.

The site's only invitation is a phone number. No “book a training.” No partner form. No newsletter. No donation button. Interested people arrive, and then leave.

The fix: one Connect page with four clear doors — partner, train, subscribe, give.
The footprint, invisible.

The national network — the single most impressive fact about Freedom, Inc. — is scattered across separate pages nobody visits.

The fix: one map, every affiliate city clickable. That screen wins meetings.

Built for every pair of eyes

This page you are reading is set the way the whole new site will be set: in Atkinson Hyperlegible — a typeface created by the Braille Institute for readers with aging and low vision — with headlines in a bold slab serif, deep contrast, and no fine print anywhere. Elders, deacons, and board members read every word without reaching for glasses. That is not decoration; for this congregation-rooted audience, it is strategy.

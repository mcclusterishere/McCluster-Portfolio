What happened — the honest record

Why the vault exists

When you enter the program, everything you make inside it signs to the corporation. That rule is what lets the cohort record, release, and get counted without anyone getting burned by a handshake. During 516R we cut originals on beats we leased but never owned — so the corporation did the disciplined thing: remade the production in-house, spent the budget on beats we could own, and released only what was ours. The original cuts — the first takes, the first energy — went in the vault, clean and waiting.

The numbers — what the market says

What buying a beat back costs

$20–$300Non-exclusive leases — what we had. Rented ground; can't build a deluxe on it.
$300–$1,000Typical exclusive license from an independent producer — the lane most of our beats sit in.
$1,000+Exclusives from producers with a name and a track record — priced by reputation, always negotiated.
Per beat, per producerThere is no fixed industry rate — every producer sets their own price, so every buy-back is its own negotiation.

The working model: at typical independent-exclusive rates, freeing the whole vault lands in the low four figures to mid four figures depending on how many producers say yes at what number. We price each beat with each producer before a dollar of the pool moves — the real quotes get posted to this round as they land, and no buy-in gets spent until the terms for its cut are locked in the Market.

The bigger machine

Trading music like positions

This round is the model for everything after it. A release isn't the end of a song's life — it's the listing. Post-release, the Market lets people take real positions in real records: buy into a remaster, a deluxe, a remix round, a video budget — and hold a recorded split that pays like the song pays. Your position is on a signed sheet, not a promise. Your M Stock ticks with every deal you close and every packet you file. The vault is the first exchange floor; the Deluxe is the first listing.
